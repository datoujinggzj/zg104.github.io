Algorithm

K-means

Summary

K-means in summarized as below:

  • Unsupervised Learning

It means we don’t have y which is the label in the training set.

  • Non-parametric

There is no prerequisite assumption about h, so it elimiate the risk of modelling based off of the distribution of the data.

For example, we are not willing to apply the LDA method to the non-Gaussian distributed data.

  • Instance-based

It means that we are not learning something from the data.

Instead, we just memorized the instances during the training process and apply them as the knowledge at the prediction stage.

  • Expensive Computation

The computation can be expensive when training on a big dataset since we must iterate over ALL the data in the dataset, which is not feasible sometimes.

So, it’s like KNN.

Core of K-means

So, the core of KNN is K exactly,too.

  • Strategy

The essence of K-means is that we try to find the centroids (all the similar points concentrate around their centroid) from the data itself to make the classification error minimized.

So, how to define the similarity?

  • Distance

Here, we use Euclidean Distance as the distance metric.

(1)d(x,x′)=(x_1−x_1′)2+...+(x_n−x_n′)2

But, we still have other options: Manhattan, Chebyshev or Hamming.

So, it’s like KNN.

  • Training Steps

Given a positive integer K, training data, and initialized k clusters C = {C1, C2, ...Ck} and a distance metric d.

  1. Sample k observations from the data to be the centroids {μ1, μ2, ...μk}.
  2. Iterate all the dataset to compute the distance between each μ and each training observation.
  1. For each observation, we assign it into the cluster of which the centroid is closest.
  1. Compute the new centroid, and do as what we do in Step 2.
  1. If the number of observations in each cluster does not change, we stop.

No tech:

  • The process begins with k centroids initialised at random.
  • These centroids are used to assign points to its nearest cluster.
  • The mean of all points within the cluster is then used to update the position of the centroids.
  • The above steps are repeated until the values of the centroids stabilise.

The complexity is O(mnk*), where m is the number of dimensions, n is the number of observations, and k is the number of clusters.

Notice: K is unknown in advance and we better don’t initialize the centroids randomly.

Choose K

The Elbow Method

At last, how to choose K?

K is a hyper-parameter to be tuned without a univerisal rule in practice.

We here use the popular method: The Elbow Method to determine the optimal number of clusters.

Calculate the Within-Cluster-Sum of Squared Errors (WSS) for different values of k, and choose the k for which WSS becomes first starts to diminish. In the plot of WSS-versus-k, this is visible as an elbow.

For example, this plot looks like an arm with a clear elbow at k = 3.

If the elbow may not be clear and sharp, what should we do to deal with such ambiguous cases?

The Silhouette Method

The silhouette value measures how similar a point is to its own cluster (cohesion) compared to other clusters (separation).

The range of the Silhouette value is between +1 and -1. A high value is desirable.

Hierarchical clustering

It is one of the popular and easy to understand clustering technique.

Strategy

  • Compute the proximity matrix
  • Make each data point a cluster
  • Repeat: Merge the 2 closest clusters and update the proximity matrix
  • Until only a single cluster remains.

Interpretation

Just like the opposite of k-means, we try to build a tree from leaves to the root.

PCA

Principal Component Analysis, or PCA, is a dimensionality-reduction method that is often used to reduce the dimensionality of large datasets, by transforming a large set of variables into a smaller one that still contains most of the information in the large set.

The dimensionality reduction is to trade a little accuracy for simplicity.

PCA can be a good visualization technique to make analyzing much easier and faster to explore.

Strategy

  1. Standardize the range of continuous initial variables
  2. Compute the covariance matrix to identify correlations
  3. Compute the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of the covariance matrix to identify the principal components
  4. Create a feature vector to decide which principal components to keep
  5. Recast the data along the principal components axes

Interpretation

We try to transform(project) the data into the orthogonal axes. And try to find the priciple components that can explain the most variances.
